For Fates







For Fates is a art project intended to collectively reimagine what a future culture of justice might be like in New York City after the close of the Rikers Island Jail Complex. The project asks people to imagine what tools, designs, and artifacts might exist to make more just communities possible. From this standpoint, they consider how these tools might actually be made a reality.
The project is based on ideation through speculative design workshops, done with a range of people affected by the justice system. The resulting designs range from the speculative to the practical. The design featured here, which was possible to instantiate in the present, was imagined by a restorative justice peacemaker at the Red Hook Community Justice Center.

Some areas suffer from poor health and also have high incarceration rates.
The skewing of the histograms, means that there are some areas with a comparatively much higher value of incarceration and poor health, which become easier to spot when visualised on a map as lighter spots (indicative of high incarceration and poor health overlap).

A preliminary analysis of the data yeilds the following correlation with incarceration.
Incarceration has a high positive correlation with assault related hospitalizations, drug related hospitalization, poverty, psychiatric hospitalization - they are all high in areas where incarceration is high.
Incarceration has a negative correlation with the number of healthcare facilities present and gross rent - these are low where incarceration is high.

We run a regression algorithm on the data, and train it to correlate all the other variables, with incarceration.
We will then look at varying the input variable of facility distribution, and see how it affects the Incarceration.
The trained model could predict accurately, the incarceration value given the other variables, 65% of the time.

We notice that in some areas, where a concentration of facilities and hospitalizations overlap, the incarceration rates are low. This lead us to try to emulate the facility density in these areas in accordance with the need.

The emulated 'Ideal Distribution of facilities', turned out to be as seen here. We then fed this into the trained regression model keeping all other variables the same and saw what changes it lead to in the incarceration pattern.

The resulting incarceration rates were reduced noticeably, especially in Bronx and Brooklyn, as per the model.

To summarize, here are the results of distributing healthcare facilities in a manner more attuned to needs, and the corresponding change in incarceration, as per the trained regression model.
Altering the number of healthcare providers available, in accordance with the need measured by hospitalization, can be seen to also affect incarceration, lowering its concentration in areas in The Bronx and Brooklyn.
